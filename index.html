Research Agenda

Overivew

The objective of my research is to understand the characteristics of human-robot interaction through information transfer between user and robot. It involves not only robot augmenting humans but also identifying the properties of the human-in-the-loop system.
My current recent research has been focused on learning the user perception via physical human-robot-interaction. Using fast-prototyping skills, mechatronics, and signal processing experience, I am exploring the minimum requirements for users to perceive and how much bandwidth the human arm-robot system can handle. Based on the physiological/psychological knowledge from human subject testing, I aim to expand on understanding more complex robot behavior through human-robot interaction.

Current Research

As pursuing my ph.D. under the supervision of Dr. Alan T. Asbeck in Virginia Tech, I developed a wearable testbed to understand the human perceptual property against kinesthetic sensations (a). The idea behind the project is to implement the kinesthetic cue as a haptic information transfer to guide/correct arm motion, just like the physical therapists aiding patients' vonlutray motion.

When Dr. Asbeck and I started Assistive Robotics Lab in Fall 2015, our high-level goal was to realize a comfortable, light-weight, and low- cost wearable device for assisting our daily life. In contrast to the full-body-support system, the device only provides partial assistance to the user, with a torque smaller than the human strength. After several prototypes of the exoskeleton, however, we realized that it would have been better if there existed any criteria on a kinesthetic cue in the scope of haptic delivery to be the guideline in designing exoskeleton for motion correction. Thus, handling the design problem into the perceptual level, rather than approaching it as finding the minimum volume of the actuator or manipulator mechanism, would be the first step toward the partial assistance system.
Developing a wearable device requires knowledge of Field Oriented Control in motor control ((b) and (c)), as well as basic human physiology as how to perceive extrinsic kinesthetic stimuli.

To accomplish the goal, we broken down the project into 1) learning psychophysical requirements for the torque output; 2) explore the torque stiffness leading to the highest bandwidth of the trainee's arm. The research has been mainly funded by the Institute for Critical Technology and Applied Science (ICTAS) scholarship.

Previous Research

During the time of an undergraduate researcher in the NYU Tandon Dynamics Systems Laboratory under Professor Maurizio Porfiri, I worked with Dr. Youngsu Cha in the energy harvesting team.

The major research topic of the team was to examine the electro- mechanical property of smart materials, specifically Piezoelectric and Ionic Polymer Metallic Composite (IPMC). Along with the fabrication of IPMCs, I built test setups for 1) underwater base excitation of the Piezoelectric composite (a), 2) underwater biomimetic shark swimming with Piezoelectric composite attached (b), and 3) impedance matching test for IPMCs (c). The data was collected with a Data Acquisition board from National Instrument and post-processed with MATLAB and Mathematica, to be compared with the calculated model.
