Recent Project

My current recent research topic is to evaluate kinesthetic feedback as another type of haptic feedback device. Haptic feedback devices are those that convey haptic information through the sense of touch. Usually, they consist of vibrating motors (like in most cell phones or video game controllers) or force-feedback joysticks (where the joystick pushes back on the user). Among thoese, kinesthetic feedback is well-known for its ability of intuitive and natural guidance, yet appropriate intensity of the kinesthetic input at which the wearer cannot be hindered is still not explored.

I am developing a paradigm of the kinesthetic cue as a means of information transfer in arm motion training without disturbing the wearer. In other words, people will move their arm in response to the applied torque, yet the applied torques are too small to disturb the motion. To accomplish the goal, I discretize the idea into two study topics: 1) when people start noticing that the device is providing force to their joint; 2) explore how sensitive trainee is to torque feedback in time and frequency domain to joint torques in terms of how fast their arm will move.

The new device I made uses a soft sleeve to apply small forces to a person's joint(a). The device should feel like a person gently guiding your arm to a certain position. The device subjects wear apply forces to the elbow of less than 5% of the maximum force that a typical adult male can generate at their elbow. Challenges of hardware design here (hardware requirements) (torque stiffness and the parasitic torque) The test result easily transferable to other exoskeleton design process for the elbow joint. Developing a wearable device that requires knowledge of Vector Control in motor control ( (b) and (c)), as well as basic human physiology as how to perceive extrinsic kinesthetic stimuli (i.e. joint proprioceptors).

Former Project

During the time of an undergraduate researcher in the NYU Tandon Dynamics Systems Laboratory, I worked in the energy harvesting team. The major research topic of the team was to examine the electro- mechanical transfer functions of smart materials, specifically Piezoelectric and Ionic Polymer Metallic Composite (IPMC).

Both IPMCs and Piezoelectric composites share the same property of generating electricity when it is deformed and actuating when it is electrified. The primary difference is the medium deliveryng those electricity. Unlike the electron charge of the Piezoelectric composite, IPMCs requires ions to transfer the electricity, and therefore only functions under ionized water. Therefore, setting the proper density of the IPMCs when fabriacting plays the key role and requires much practice to make a successful materials.

All the experimental setup I designed was to investigate/measure the smart materials in oscillatory motion, then to be analyzed in the time and frequency domain in terms of transfer functions. It includes: 1) underwater base excitation of the Piezoelectric composite (a), 2) underwater biomimetic shark swimming with Piezoelectric composite attached (b), and 3) impedance matching test for IPMCs (c).

The data was collected with a Data Acquisition board from National Instrument and high-speed camera, then post-processed with MATLAB and Mathematica, to be compared with the theoretical model.
